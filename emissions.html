CO2 EMISSIONS AND TEMPERATURE

By Silvia Ferrer and Daniella Escobar

The main objective of this project was to understand the relationship between CO2 emissions, temperature, and time. We also wanted to see a breakdown by country to understand how these countries have contributed to global emissions.

We combined 2 datasets from Kaggle: One contained global emissions data from the years 1750–2022, and went into the details of the emission source, like oil, gas, flaring, or coal. The other dataset provided global temperature data from roughly the same years and listed the average temperature per month.

Our research questions focus on the relationship between CO2 emissions, time, temperature, and countries.

Let’s start with the relationship between time, temperature, and time and total emissions in the past century.

These two graphs show trend lines for the impact of time on the total global emissions and average temperature. In these graphs, time explains 99 and 44% of the variability in emissions and temperature, respectively.

We can see that CO2 emissions have increased exponentially in the past decades, while temperature steadily increased with a lower slope. However, in recent years, we have not reached the lower average temperatures from those during the 20th century. Now, let’s see how this increase in emissions through time gets distributed to the whole world.

The following graph shows the trend line between the sum of total emissions in the world each year and the average temperature in the world. Since the sum of total emissions have been constantly rising each year, I am using it as a proxy of time as well. So not only does this model describe the relationship between emissions and time, but time as well. We can see a positive relation between the two of those, and with a R squared of 0.63, the model says that emissions explain 63% of the increase in temperature each year. Its equation is: ln(Avg. Average Temperature) = 0.0652911*ln(Total Emissions) + 2.07176 A 95% CI of the graph would consider any data point 0.007782 Celsius degrees away from the trend.

For these graphs, we divided time into 3 periods: The first industrial revolution from 1750 to 1850, the second industrial revolution from 1850 to 1950, and the third and fourth industrial revolutions from 1950 to present days. We put these last two together (third and fourth) because there were not enough data points from the Fourth Industrial Revolution to make a fair comparison. Also, there wasn’t much of a difference between the first and second industrial revolutions in the map, but there was a dramatic shift between the first and third. During the first phase, most nations were still agrarian or pre‑industrial; they lacked large‑scale coal use and rail transportation, so their emissions stayed minimal throughout the First and Second Industrial Revolutions. In comparison, England pioneered the steam engine, which allowed it to mechanize and fuel energy‑intensive factories, pushing its CO₂ totals far above every other country. In the third and fourth periods, Large emerging economies (China, India, Brazil) and export‑led manufacturing shifted the spread of CO₂ output beyond Europe, amplifying global totals and reshaping the emissions leaderboard. These countries make the top contributors in this phase.
